Overview

After you go through our guide on creating resumes and cover letters you will be able to apply for jobs and sooner than later you will start getting invitations to interviews! This is awesome and it feels great to land your first interview, especially with a company you really respect, but as great as landing an interview is it feels even better to actually get the job! How? You practice as much as you can, preferably for at least a month before you start getting interviewed. If you are applying for a technical position at a well known company, you will likely go through an interview in which they will ask you technical questions. Depending on both the company and the position the questions range in difficulty and content, but regardless of the specific questions it will be incredibly helpful if you practice. How exactly do you practice? Easy - I have laid it all out for you! Firstly, make sure you understand the Problem Solving Design Pattern so that you can apply it when you are solving difficult questions. Next, quickly read my practice tips to make sure that you get the most out of your time. Lastly, check out the resources listed and start practicing with whatever resources interest you most! Best of luck!

Problem Solving Design Pattern (PSDP)

When asked to solve a question in an interview, a common mistake, a mistake I made myself for quite a long time, is to jump into solving the question immediately. Surprisingly, this is not the best approach! There are important steps that if followed correctly can give you a large advantage. To make it as easy as possible, I have clearly stated each step below.

  1. Understand It

    You want to make sure that you clearly understand the problem statement. Having the perfect solution to the wrong problem isn't going to impress the interviewer. As well, questions in interviews are sometimes asked with details missing purposefully; they do this to see if you can identify and resolve the ambiguities. So ask questions and make sure you completely understand the problem!

  2. Plan It

    If possible, write out the signatures of the methods you will be implementing, this often helps when you get confused half-way through a solution. If you are asked to implement a class of some sort it may also be helpful to write a tiny API for that class (essentially a bunch of method signatures). The point of this stage is to build a skeleton for your solution.

  3. Test It

    Think about possible inputs into your method and the results. Remember to look at your signatures when coming up with inputs/outputs to make sure you are using the correct types. When thinking of inputs, try and cover all the normal cases and edge cases. You'll be thankful when you are trying to solve the problem because it is very helpful to have tests with expected outputs (especially when getting lost in a solution).

  4. Solve It

    Finally, you get to actually solve the problem. At this point you should clearly understand the problem, have the signature for the method you are about to implement, and understand the various edge cases and their expected results. When actually solving the problem, there is no need for code. You should be using pseudo-code and diagrams to make sure you have a good solution. Is your solution fast enough? Does it take too much space? These are good questions to ask yourself while you are solving the problem. If you come up with a "slow" solution but you cannot come up with a "fast" solution, do not panic - move on to the next stage and code your slow solution while talking to the interviewer about the potential weaknesses in the algorithm and the places it could improve.

  5. Code It

    Yay you actually get to write some code! At this point you have your solution in pseudo-code so implement your solution. If you forgot the names of standard methods it is not a big deal, do not get caught up in whether it is "string".size( ) or "string".length( ), instead focus on fully implementing your solution. If you forget the name of a standard method simply use a logical name in its place (length or size are fine, your interviewer isn't going to mind that you haven't memorized the standard library).

  6. Check It

    You thought you were done! Not quite! Look over your solution and run your tests in your head and see if you get back the expected results. It is much better to have a bug in your code and find it yourself than to have your interviewer find it for you. If you find any bugs simply point them out and try to fix them.

Practice Tips

There are just a few important tips I wanted to leave with you before you start practicing for your interviews.

  1. Do not use an IDE while you practice! The best ways to practice solving interview questions is in the exact same environment that you will be doing the interview itself. If you will be interviewing with Google over the phone, then go ahead and practice writing code in a Google Doc. If you are going to be going to the company itself for the interview, practice on a whiteboard or on paper if you don't have access to a board. If you hate coding without an IDE because you love auto-complete, that's all the more reason to practice without one! You will not have auto-complete in the interview.

  2. Follow the PSDP while practicing for interviews. If you don't follow the PSDP when you are practicing, you are not going to use it during your interview either. This includes the final step, going through your code and trying to debug it; it is important not to get lazy during your training and immediately plug your solutions into the computer before debugging them in your head. Always try and check if your solutions are correct mentally before plugging them into a computer. You can choose to ignore the PSDP, something I did myself for quite a while, but I saw a massive increase in my performance when I started using it. It is particularly helpful when dealing with a challenging problem where you do not immediately know the answer.

  3. Practice with a friend. It can be very stressful to work on a problem while someone is watching over your shoulder, but unfortunately this is exactly what the interview is going to be like. To prepare yourself, ask one of your friends who is also a CS student to interview you. If you have a friend who is also preparing for interviews, then rotate being the interviewer/interviewee. For your mock interviews you can use questions from any of the resources listed below.

Resources

  1. Cracking the Coding Interview Book

    The author, Gayle Laakmann McDowell, is an incredibly talented programmer who has devoted a lot of her time towards understanding technical interviews. She has worked at many top companies such as Microsoft, Apple, and Google; at Google she was one of the lead interviewers. Her book, "Cracking the Coding Interview", is perhaps the most well known book when it comes to practicing for technical interviews, and it's well known for a reason - it's a great book. Getting this book and working through the problems in any language is an excellent way to practice! It has about 200 problems so it will keep you busy for a long time!

  2. Interview Cake Website

    A small manageable set of problems that are designed to efficiently help you prepare for technical interviews. By the time you are done the ~45 problems, you are meant to be prepared for a wide range of technical interview questions. What's nice about this website is that there are multiple hints for each question that guide you towards the correct answer, as well as clear detailed solutions to help you understand each step.

  3. Project Euler Website

    An amazing way to practice for interviews and build up your critical thinking skills. It is an archive of Computer Science / Math problems of varying difficulty. Many of the questions in this archive are far more difficult than the questions you will encounter in an interview so they can be an amazing way to practice. I do not recommend only practicing with Project Euler because it is important to practice easier interview-level questions, but I definitely think this is a great resource to strengthen the general skills you need for technical interviews!

  4. Interview Practice Github Repository

    This is a repository that I started when I was applying for Google to keep track of all my preparation. Slowly the repository expanded, and then I decided instead of just keeping track of my preparation I would put in the extra effort and turn it into a guide. First it goes over the implementation of numerous data structures and sorts followed by a series of interview problems. All of the data structures, sorts, and problems are implemented in Java in my "answer key". I highly recommend to check out this repository and follow the guide in the repository, it'll tell you exactly what you need to do!
